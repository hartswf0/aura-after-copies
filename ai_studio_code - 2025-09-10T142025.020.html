Axiom 2: Mark

Axioms

The first act of distinction: A Mark appears

A Mark is a cut in the temporal stream, creating an 'event' or a 'memory'. This single distinction creates two sides: 'this side' and 'that side'.

Example: A timestamp in a log file; the first recorded memory in an archive.

Marking creates an inside and an outside

Axiom 3: Unmarked

Axioms

The dual nature of distinction: Marked and Unmarked

Every mark creates both a 'marked' point and 'unmarked' stretches around it. These unmarked areas are potential for new distinctions, or forgotten states of being.

Example: A moment of 'flow state' (unmarked) broken by a notification (mark).

The silence between notes is just as important

Axiom 4: Crossing

Axioms

The operation that transforms marked ↔ unmarked spaces

Crossing occurs when two marks interact, collapsing into an unmarked state. This can be forgetting (marked to unmarked) or remembrance (unmarked to marked), shaping our temporal reality.

Example: Two adjacent tasks on a to-do list get combined into one bigger, unmarked project.
